\begin{abstract}
Deep-rooting perennial biofuel cropping systems offer a promising carbon-sink strategy. These crops capture atmospheric carbon dioxide, sequester it in plants and soil, and displace fossil fuel with bioethanol produced from crop biomass. This study determines geo-specific regional carbon sequestration potential from land-use change in the Permian Basin area, converting conventional land covers to perennial biofuel cropping systems under a range of management strategies. DAYCENT, a calibrated biogeochemical model, simulates carbon and nitrogen dynamics aboveground and belowground under switchgrass and miscanthus production with different nitrogen fertilization, irrigation, and harvest strategies. The analysis evaluates aboveground biomass, soil organic carbon and nutrients, and greenhouse gas fluxes (nitrous oxide and methane) across 16 management scenarios layered on three starting land covers (barren, shrubland, and rainfed cropland). The study reports net carbon change—including indirect nitrous oxide emissions from ammonia and nitric oxide volatilization and nitrate leaching—to identify the management combinations that maximize on-field carbon outcomes. The findings highlight the best-performing combinations of land cover and management for agronomic and environmental performance, and the results inform integrated assessment models that take additional system components into account.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} land use change, soil organic carbon, soil health, DAYCENT, Permian Basin

Anthropogenic land use has significantly depleted the global soil organic carbon (SOC) pool, and restoring it represents a crucial strategy for mitigating the worst impacts of climate change \citep{Lal2001SoilSequestration,Lal2004bClimateChange,Sanderman2017SoilCarbonDebt,Scharlemann2014GlobalSoilCarbon}. Arid lands cover 45\% of Earth's land surface and store 15\% of global SOC, which makes them both an opportunity and a challenge for SOC restoration \citep{Lal2004aDrylandEcosystems,Schimel2010Drylands}. Establishing high-biomass vegetation in these regions enhances net productivity and helps replenish SOC, and field studies show that transitioning barren land and shrubland to higher-biomass vegetation—such as grasslands or perennial crops—increases carbon sequestration \citep{Arije2024SOCRecovery}. Adopting sustainable land-management practices in cultivated areas, such as conservation agriculture or cover cropping, also raises carbon sequestration potential by promoting higher biomass production and increasing soil organic carbon \citep{Farage2007Placeholder,He2025Placeholder,Joshi2023Placeholder,Moukanni2022Placeholder}. Limited moisture, alkaline soils that constrain plant growth, and high winds that erode soil and damage non-woody vegetation all hamper revegetation or agricultural operations in arid lands \citep{DOdorico2012Placeholder,Naorem2023Placeholder}. These conditions make marginal land-use change an opportunity to promote higher-biomass vegetation and enhance carbon sequestration.

Perennial biofuel crops play an important role in reducing carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions both in the field and across the life cycle of biofuel production. Miscanthus (Miscanthus x giganteus) and switchgrass make especially good candidates for cultivation on marginal lands and outperform conventional biofuel crops such as corn on several measures of agricultural sustainability. First, their lower fertilizer and water requirements reduce the carbon footprint of agricultural practices \citep{Davis2009Placeholder,Zeri2013WUE}. Second, both miscanthus and switchgrass improve SOC sequestration by slowing soil carbon mineralization rates, whereas corn cultivation depletes SOC \citep{AndersonTeixeira2009SOC,Ferrarini2022Placeholder}. Third, land-use conversion to miscanthus or switchgrass reduces soil erosion because perennial crops provide year-round ground cover, which preserves topsoil and the organic matter critical for carbon sequestration \citep{Ferrarini2022Placeholder,Smeets2009Performance}. Together, these traits make miscanthus and switchgrass in drylands an effective strategy for reducing the atmospheric concentration of greenhouse gases on three fronts: mitigating soil carbon loss from erosion, enhancing SOC sequestration, and replacing conventional fuels with lower-emitting biofuels.

Converting marginal land covers to perennial biofuel crops therefore offers a promising approach for mitigating greenhouse-gas emissions. Shifting land use from agriculture, grazing, or non-native grasslands to perennial crops like switchgrass, miscanthus, or hybrid poplar engages deep root systems and extensive below-ground biomass that capture carbon and store it as long-term soil organic carbon \citep{Johnson2007BiomassCropping,Davis2012Agave}. The harvested biomass also supports biofuel production—capturing and storing carbon while promoting biodiversity and ecosystem health \citep{VanDerWeijde2017Drought,Zalesny2020Placeholder}. Given the long-term viability and sustained sequestration potential of perennial biofuel crops, the Permian Basin offers a significant opportunity to contribute to carbon-sequestration efforts \citep{Kaye2018SoilCarbon}. Successful implementation, however, depends on land suitability, water availability, economic feasibility, and comprehensive planning and policy \citep{Field2017Placeholder}. Economically feasible and sustainable miscanthus and switchgrass cultivation therefore hinges on land suitability and moisture availability.

This study proceeds in two main steps. First, the analysis assembles the inputs that DAYCENT requires for regional simulations at 1 km resolution, assuming that land-use change begins in 2021 and continues through 2100. DAYCENT (Daily Century), developed by \citet{Parton1998DAYCENT}, is a widely used process-based biogeochemical model that simulates the complex interactions between climate, soil, and vegetation in terrestrial ecosystems. The model serves as a valuable tool for studying carbon and nitrogen dynamics and for assessing how land-management practices and climate change affect ecosystem processes. DAYCENT takes climate data, soil information, vegetation parameters, and land-management practices as inputs and produces net primary production, soil organic carbon dynamics, nitrogen mineralization, leaching, and greenhouse gas emissions as outputs \citep{DelGrosso2005Placeholder,Izaurralde2006EPIC}. This enables researchers and policymakers to evaluate different scenarios and make informed decisions about sustainable land management. Numerous studies have validated and applied the model \citep{Davis2009Placeholder,DelGrosso2006NationalScale,DelGrosso2005Placeholder,Jarecki2020Placeholder,Laub2024Placeholder}, establishing it as a reliable and robust tool for ecological research and decision-making. DAYMET (1980-2020, repeated through 2100) from DOE ORNL supplies weather inputs. gSSURGO from USDA NRCS supplies soil-property inputs. The USDA NASS Cropland Data Layer (CDL) supplies the starting land-cover classification.

\subsubsection*{Comparison between land management scenarios}

The Permian Basin's arid climate and water scarcity make irrigation a paramount factor in agricultural success, often outweighing the role of nitrogen application. The region's limited rainfall and high evaporation rates \citep{Gutzler1998Placeholder} create a precarious moisture balance, so irrigation typically determines whether crops receive enough water \citep{Djaman2009Placeholder}. Many crops grown in the Permian Basin, such as cotton and certain grains, demand substantial water \citep{Djaman2009Placeholder}, and without irrigation they rarely achieve optimal yields or quality. High soil salinity in some areas further requires proper irrigation practice for effective salinity management \citep{Eaton2009IrrigationSystems}. Nitrogen remains essential for plant growth, but its efficacy depends on water availability, and inadequate moisture limits nutrient uptake \citep{Kaur2016Placeholder}. In this water-scarce environment, irrigation therefore takes priority over nitrogen fertilization.

Miscanthus (Miscanthus x giganteus) typically holds higher soil organic carbon (SOC) than switchgrass (Panicum virgatum) because miscanthus produces more below-ground biomass, its lignocellulosic residues decompose more slowly, and its root system sequesters carbon efficiently \citep{Gelfand2013MarginalLands,Hastings2018Placeholder}. Miscanthus's larger and more densely packed growth habit yields more root biomass and greater root turnover, which together increase SOC inputs \citep{Gelfand2013MarginalLands,Xu2024Placeholder}. The high lignin content in miscanthus biomass also slows decomposition rates and promotes carbon retention in soil \citep{Hastings2018Placeholder}.

Switchgrass produces more fine-root biomass than miscanthus, yet its total below-ground biomass is about half that of miscanthus \citep{Chimento2015Placeholder,Martani2021Placeholder}. Water stress may further reduce switchgrass below-ground biomass more than it reduces miscanthus's because of switchgrass's lower water-use efficiency \citep{VanLoocke2012WUE,Zeri2013WUE}. Below-ground biomass also decomposes more slowly in miscanthus than in switchgrass.

These factors collectively enhance SOC accumulation in miscanthus fields relative to switchgrass and highlight miscanthus's promise as a bioenergy crop for carbon sequestration and soil health.

Leaving aboveground biomass unharvested—a practice commonly known as crop-residue retention—raises soil organic carbon (SOC) even without tillage through several interrelated mechanisms. Unharvested plant residues act as a continual source of organic matter, gradually decomposing and enriching SOC \citep{Mann2012Placeholder}. Residues on the soil surface also protect against wind and water erosion, which reduces loss of existing SOC \citep{BlancoCanqui2016Placeholder}. The residues further serve as a substrate for soil microorganisms, stimulating microbial activity and accelerating incorporation of organic carbon into the soil \citep{Lal2005ForestSoils}. By preserving and replenishing soil organic carbon, crop-residue retention improves soil fertility, structure, and water-holding capacity, and contributes to climate-change mitigation through carbon sequestration.

Higher inputs of irrigation and nitrogen fertilization also raise soil organic carbon (SOC) through several mechanisms. Irrigation enhances plant growth and productivity, which increases plant biomass inputs to the soil \citep{Wang2018Placeholder}. The additional organic matter—from root biomass and crop residues—provides a substrate for microbial activity and accelerates incorporation of organic carbon into the soil \citep{Basso2013Placeholder}. Nitrogen fertilization stimulates plant growth in a similar way, which transfers more organic carbon to the soil through root exudates and crop residues \citep{Liu2016Placeholder}. Improved plant growth and vigor under nitrogen fertilization also build root biomass, which contributes to SOC accumulation. Together, these inputs augment SOC content and improve soil fertility and carbon sequestration.

\subsection*{N2O emissions}

\manufig{figures/figure6.png}{Figure 6. Supporting outcomes at year 2100. Distributions of N2O flux and biomass yield by management scenario, coloured by starting land cover.}

Nitrogen fertilization raises nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions more than irrigation does, through distinct mechanisms. Nitrogen fertilizers add nitrogen to the soil, which boosts microbial activity and increases nitrogen availability for the nitrification and denitrification reactions that produce N2O as a byproduct \citep{Bouwman1996Placeholder,Bouwman2002Placeholder,ButterbachBahl2013Placeholder,LiuGreaver2009Placeholder}. Irrigation, by contrast, can lower N2O emissions by creating conditions that favor microbial reduction of N2O to dinitrogen gas (N2) under anaerobic conditions \citep{Groffman2009Placeholder}. Well-timed irrigation also regulates soil moisture and limits excessive nitrogen availability, which further reduces N2O production \citep{Zhu2013Placeholder}. These contrasting effects highlight the need for a balanced approach when managing nitrogen fertilization and irrigation to mitigate N2O emissions.

No-harvest or unharvested fields typically emit more nitrous oxide (N2O) than harvested fields because organic residues accumulate and soil organic matter rises. When crop residues remain on the field, they supply a continuous source of organic carbon. The resulting organic matter, together with residual nitrogen in the crop residues, creates conditions that favor denitrification in anaerobic microsites and elevates N2O emissions \citep{Smith2008GHGMitigation}. Harvesting, by contrast, removes a significant portion of the organic residues, which reduces the substrate available for denitrification and, in turn, lowers N2O emissions. Multiple studies have observed this effect and emphasize the importance of harvest practice for managing greenhouse-gas emissions in agricultural systems.

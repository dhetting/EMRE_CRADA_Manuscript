\title{Soil health outcomes and carbon dynamics under land-use change to perennial biofuel crops in the Permian Basin}
\author{Yong Wang \and Paul Pinchuk \and Rebecca Hanes \and Patrick Lamers \and Dylan Hettinger}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Deep-rooting perennial biofuel crops improve soil health by building soil organic carbon (SOC), enhancing soil fertility, and protecting against erosion on marginal and degraded lands. This study quantifies geo-specific SOC sequestration potential from land-use change in the Permian Basin, converting barren land, native shrubland, and rainfed cropland to miscanthus (\textit{Miscanthus} $\times$ \textit{giganteus}) or switchgrass (\textit{Panicum virgatum}) under 16 management strategies defined by combinations of nitrogen fertilization, irrigation, and harvest regime. DAYCENT, a calibrated process-based biogeochemical model, simulates SOC dynamics, biomass productivity, and soil nitrogen cycling at 1 km resolution from 2021 through 2100. Across all 48 scenario–land-cover combinations, mean SOC increases by 14 to 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ by year 2100, with the greatest gains under miscanthus with irrigation and residue retention on shrubland. Moisture availability is the primary management lever: irrigated scenarios consistently exceed rainfed counterparts in both SOC accumulation and biomass yield. A pronounced spatial gradient follows the regional rainfall pattern, with the wetter southern basin offering substantially greater soil-health benefits than the drier north. These results identify the crop–management combinations that most effectively restore soil health in this semi-arid region and provide a regional map of SOC sequestration potential to guide land-use planning.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} land use change, soil organic carbon, soil health, DAYCENT, Permian Basin

Anthropogenic land use has significantly depleted the global soil organic carbon (SOC) pool, and restoring it is a central strategy for improving soil productivity, biological activity, and long-term land health \citep{Lal2001SoilSequestration,Lal2004bClimateChange,Sanderman2017SoilCarbonDebt,Scharlemann2014GlobalSoilCarbon}. Arid lands cover 45\% of Earth's land surface and store 15\% of global SOC, which makes them both an opportunity and a challenge for SOC restoration \citep{Lal2004aDrylandEcosystems,Schimel2010Drylands}. Establishing high-biomass vegetation in these regions enhances net primary productivity and helps replenish SOC, and field studies show that transitioning barren land and shrubland to higher-biomass vegetation—such as grasslands or perennial crops—increases SOC and improves soil fertility \citep{Arije2024SOCRecovery}. Adopting sustainable land-management practices in cultivated areas, such as conservation agriculture or cover cropping, further raises SOC by promoting higher biomass production \citep{Farage2007Placeholder,He2025Placeholder,Joshi2023Placeholder,Moukanni2022Placeholder}. Limited moisture, alkaline soils that constrain plant growth, and high winds that erode soil and damage non-woody vegetation all hamper revegetation or agricultural operations in arid lands \citep{DOdorico2012Placeholder,Naorem2023Placeholder}. These conditions make marginal land-use change an opportunity to promote higher-biomass vegetation and restore soil health.


Perennial biofuel crops that are well adapted to water-limited conditions—such as miscanthus and switchgrass—can thrive on marginal lands like those of the Permian Basin, where deep root systems build SOC while producing harvestable biomass for bioenergy \citep{Zeri2013WUE,VanLoocke2012WUE,Gelfand2013MarginalLands}.

Miscanthus (\textit{Miscanthus} $\times$ \textit{giganteus}) and switchgrass are especially well suited for cultivation on marginal lands and outperform conventional biofuel crops such as corn on several measures of soil health and agricultural sustainability. First, their lower fertilizer and water requirements reduce input intensity on already nutrient-limited soils \citep{Davis2009Placeholder,Zeri2013WUE}. Second, both miscanthus and switchgrass improve SOC by slowing soil carbon mineralization rates, whereas corn cultivation depletes SOC \citep{AndersonTeixeira2009SOC,Ferrarini2022Placeholder}. Third, land-use conversion to miscanthus or switchgrass reduces soil erosion because perennial crops provide year-round ground cover, preserving topsoil and the organic matter critical for soil fertility \citep{Ferrarini2022Placeholder,Smeets2009Performance}. Together, these traits make miscanthus and switchgrass in drylands an effective strategy for improving soil health—restoring SOC, building soil structure, and protecting against erosion \citep{Zeri2013WUE,VanLoocke2012WUE}.

Converting marginal land covers to perennial biofuel crops therefore offers a promising approach for restoring soil health and improving land productivity. Shifting land use from degraded agriculture, sparse shrubland, or bare land to perennial crops like switchgrass or miscanthus engages deep root systems and extensive below-ground biomass that build long-term SOC \citep{Johnson2007BiomassCropping,Davis2012Agave}. The harvested biomass simultaneously supports biofuel production, providing an economic incentive for land conversion that also benefits biodiversity and ecosystem function \citep{VanDerWeijde2017Drought,Zalesny2020Placeholder}. Successful implementation depends on land suitability and water availability; economically feasible miscanthus and switchgrass cultivation hinges on local moisture conditions \citep{Field2017Placeholder,Kaye2018SoilCarbon}.

As a case in point, the Permian Basin encompasses barren land, native shrubland, and rainfed cropland—three starting land covers that span a wide range of initial SOC states and present different magnitudes of potential SOC gain under conversion to perennial biofuel crops.

In this study, biogeochemical modeling evaluates regional SOC sequestration potential and soil health outcomes across a range of management strategies and starting land covers in the Permian Basin.

Soils vary across the basin but commonly include caliche, sandy soils, clay-rich soils, and saline soils, each of which poses distinct challenges for agriculture and land use \citep{EatonSalinityPlaceholder}.

\subsection*{Analytical workflow}

This study proceeds in two main steps. First, the analysis assembles the inputs that DAYCENT requires for regional simulations at 1 km resolution, assuming that land-use change begins in 2021 and continues through 2100. Second, the analysis applies a single factorial study design—2 crops $\times$ 2 fertilization levels $\times$ 2 irrigation regimes $\times$ 2 harvest regimes = 16 management scenarios—to each of three starting land covers retained for this paper: barren, shrubland, and rainfed cropland (\texttt{cultivated\_rfd}). Figure 2 summarizes the spatial distribution of the starting land covers across the Permian Basin and the factorial structure of the 16 scenarios.

DAYCENT (Daily Century), developed by \citet{Parton1998DAYCENT}, is a widely used process-based biogeochemical model that simulates the interactions among climate, soil, and vegetation in terrestrial ecosystems. The model is well suited for studying soil carbon and nitrogen dynamics and for assessing how land-management practices affect ecosystem processes and soil health. DAYCENT takes climate data, soil information, vegetation parameters, and land-management practices as inputs and produces net primary production, soil organic carbon dynamics, nitrogen mineralization, leaching, and trace-gas fluxes as outputs \citep{DelGrosso2005Placeholder,Izaurralde2006EPIC}. Numerous studies have validated and applied the model across a range of agroecosystem contexts \citep{Davis2009Placeholder,DelGrosso2006NationalScale,DelGrosso2005Placeholder,Jarecki2020Placeholder,Laub2024Placeholder}, establishing it as a reliable tool for evaluating land-management scenarios. DAYMET daily gridded meteorological data (1980--2020, repeated through 2100) from DOE ORNL supplies weather inputs \citep{Thornton2014Placeholder}. Soil-property inputs are extracted from the gridded Soil Survey Geographic Database (gSSURGO) produced by USDA NRCS \citep{NRCSgSSURGOPlaceholder}. The starting land-cover classification is derived from the USDA NASS Cropland Data Layer (CDL) \citep{NASCDLPlaceholder}.

\section*{Results and discussion}

Results summary: Miscanthus outperforms switchgrass across all soil health metrics and agronomic outcomes. The primary differentiating factor is moisture availability: irrigated scenarios consistently yield greater SOC accumulation and higher biomass productivity than rainfed counterparts across all three starting land covers.

\subsection*{Soil organic carbon sequestration}

\manufig{figures/figure3.png}{Figure 3. Change in soil organic carbon by management scenario and starting land cover. Box-and-whisker distributions of $\Delta$SOC at years 2030, 2050, and 2100; boxes show 25th/50th/75th percentiles, whiskers extend to 1.5$\times$IQR.}

\manufig{figures/figure4.png}{Figure 4. Spatial pattern of $\Delta$SOC at year 2100, faceted by management scenario. Pixels are on the 1 km grid covering the Permian Basin extent.}

SOC rises in all scenarios (Figs. 3 and 4), consistent with prior reports \citep{Dohleman2009Miscanthus,Iqbal2015Placeholder,Jarecki2020Placeholder} that perennial biofuel crops sequester SOC relative to degraded or cultivated baselines. Across all 48 scenario–land-cover combinations simulated here, mean $\Delta$SOC at year 2100 ranges from 14 to 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$, with the highest sequestration rates under miscanthus cultivation on shrubland with irrigation and residue retention.

Miscanthus (\textit{Miscanthus} $\times$ \textit{giganteus}) typically accumulates more SOC than switchgrass (\textit{Panicum virgatum}) because miscanthus produces more below-ground biomass, its lignocellulosic residues decompose more slowly, and its root system contributes organic carbon to deeper soil layers \citep{Gelfand2013MarginalLands,Hastings2018Placeholder}. Miscanthus's larger and more densely packed growth habit yields more root biomass and greater root turnover, which together increase SOC inputs \citep{Gelfand2013MarginalLands,Xu2024Placeholder}. The high lignin content in miscanthus biomass also slows decomposition rates and promotes carbon retention in soil \citep{Hastings2018Placeholder}.

Switchgrass produces more fine-root biomass than miscanthus, yet its total below-ground biomass is about half that of miscanthus \citep{Chimento2015Placeholder,Martani2021Placeholder}. Water stress may further reduce switchgrass below-ground biomass more than miscanthus because of switchgrass's lower water-use efficiency \citep{VanLoocke2012WUE,Zeri2013WUE}. Below-ground biomass also decomposes more slowly in miscanthus than in switchgrass, providing a longer-lasting SOC input \citep{Hastings2018Placeholder,Xu2024Placeholder}.

These factors collectively enhance SOC accumulation in miscanthus fields relative to switchgrass and underscore miscanthus's potential as a high-impact crop for soil-health restoration on marginal lands.

Leaving aboveground biomass unharvested—a practice commonly known as crop-residue retention—raises SOC through several interrelated mechanisms. Unharvested plant residues act as a continual source of organic matter, gradually decomposing and enriching SOC \citep{Mann2012Placeholder}. Residues on the soil surface protect against wind and water erosion, reducing loss of existing SOC and preserving the physical structure of the topsoil \citep{BlancoCanqui2016Placeholder}. The residues further serve as a substrate for soil microorganisms, stimulating microbial activity and accelerating incorporation of organic carbon into stable soil aggregates \citep{Lal2005ForestSoils,SixPlaceholder2002}. By preserving and replenishing SOC, crop-residue retention improves soil fertility, water-holding capacity, and aggregate stability \citep{BlancoCanaquisPlaceholder2011}.

Higher inputs of irrigation and nitrogen fertilization also raise SOC through stimulation of plant growth. Irrigation enhances productivity, which increases above- and below-ground biomass inputs to the soil \citep{Wang2018Placeholder}. The additional organic matter—root biomass and crop residues—provides substrate for microbial activity and accelerates the incorporation of carbon into stable soil fractions \citep{Basso2013Placeholder}. Nitrogen fertilization stimulates plant growth similarly, transferring more organic carbon to the soil through root exudates and residues \citep{Liu2016Placeholder}. Together, these inputs augment SOC and improve soil fertility.

\subsection*{SOC accumulation over time}

\manufig{figures/figure5.png}{Figure 5. $\Delta$SOC trajectory by scenario and starting land cover across years 2030, 2050, and 2100. Lines show the mean across pixels; ribbons span the 25th--75th percentile.}

The rate of SOC accumulation is highest early in the simulation and declines over time as the soil carbon pool approaches a new equilibrium (Fig. 5). Early accumulation is driven by the transition from low-productivity baseline land covers to high-biomass perennial systems, which rapidly increases organic-matter inputs. As the pool deepens, labile carbon fractions—those that turn over on timescales of months to a few years—cycle more rapidly and contribute less to net SOC gain, while recalcitrant fractions accumulate more slowly but persist longer \citep{Parton1987Placeholder,VonLutzow2007Placeholder}. This shift in pool composition explains the characteristic flattening of the SOC trajectory across all scenarios by mid-century.

Management strategies that maintain high and continuous biomass input—particularly irrigation and residue retention—delay the onset of this plateau and sustain elevated SOC accumulation rates through 2100 (Fig. 5).

\subsubsection*{Comparison between land management scenarios}

The Permian Basin's arid climate and water scarcity make irrigation a paramount factor in biomass productivity, often outweighing the role of nitrogen fertilization. The region's limited rainfall and high evaporation rates \citep{Gutzler1998Placeholder} create a precarious moisture balance, so irrigation typically determines whether crops receive adequate water for growth \citep{Djaman2009Placeholder}. High soil salinity in some areas further requires proper irrigation practice for effective salinity management and root-zone health \citep{Eaton2009IrrigationSystems}. Nitrogen remains essential for plant growth, but its efficacy depends on water availability, and inadequate soil moisture limits nutrient uptake and root exploration \citep{Kaur2016Placeholder}. In this water-scarce environment, irrigation therefore takes priority over nitrogen fertilization as a determinant of yield.

Miscanthus (\textit{Miscanthus} $\times$ \textit{giganteus}) achieves higher yields than switchgrass across all land-management scenarios, in agreement with studies spanning diverse agroecological contexts \citep{Chen2019Placeholder,Dohleman2009Miscanthus,Iqbal2015Placeholder,Jarecki2020Placeholder,Khanna2008Placeholder}. Several factors drive this yield gap. First, miscanthus uses less water per unit of biomass \citep{VanLoocke2012WUE,Zeri2013WUE}. Second, its lower nutrient requirements suit less fertile soils, while its longer growing season and greater biomass density boost productivity \citep{Dohleman2009Miscanthus,Iqbal2015Placeholder}. In addition, miscanthus shows reduced susceptibility to pests and diseases, allocates carbon efficiently to above-ground biomass, tolerates drought stress, and persists across years without annual re-establishment—all of which compound its yield advantage \citep{Chen2019Placeholder,Heaton2010MiscanthusPromising,Zeri2013WUE}.

\subsection*{Soil nitrogen dynamics}

Nitrogen fertilization raises soil nitrous oxide (N2O) flux—a direct measure of nitrogen loss from the soil—more than irrigation does, through distinct mechanisms. Nitrogen fertilizers increase soil nitrogen availability, boosting microbial nitrification and denitrification activity that converts mineral nitrogen to N2O as a byproduct \citep{Bouwman1996Placeholder,Bouwman2002Placeholder,ButterbachBahl2013Placeholder,LiuGreaver2009Placeholder}. Irrigation, by contrast, can moderate N2O flux by maintaining more uniform soil moisture conditions that reduce the anaerobic microsites where denitrification is concentrated \citep{Groffman2009Placeholder,Zhu2013Placeholder}. From a soil-nitrogen-retention perspective, management strategies that minimize fertilizer inputs while maintaining adequate soil moisture are most effective at keeping nitrogen in the soil and available to plants.

No-harvest management retains crop residues on the soil surface, which increases soil organic matter and creates conditions that elevate nitrogen turnover. The organic matter and residual nitrogen in unharvested residues provide substrate for denitrifying bacteria in anaerobic microsites, increasing N2O flux relative to harvested scenarios \citep{Smith2008GHGMitigation,VerhoefPlaceholder2006}. Harvesting removes a significant portion of the organic residues, limiting the denitrification substrate and thus retaining more nitrogen in plant-available mineral forms. The trade-off is that harvesting also removes organic matter that would otherwise contribute to longer-term SOC accumulation, so the optimal harvest decision depends on management priorities—maximizing soil nitrogen retention versus maximizing SOC buildup \citep{BlancoCanqui2016Placeholder}.

The spatial pattern of N2O flux broadly mirrors that of SOC accumulation: wetter southern portions of the basin show higher N2O flux under fertilized scenarios, reflecting greater overall microbial activity and soil nitrogen cycling in more productive environments.

\section*{Conclusions}

Land-use conversion to miscanthus or switchgrass consistently and substantially builds soil organic carbon in the Permian Basin, regardless of management strategy or starting land cover. Across all 48 scenario–land-cover combinations simulated over an 80-year horizon, mean $\Delta$SOC at year 2100 ranges from 14 to 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$, demonstrating that converting degraded or underproductive marginal lands to perennial biofuel crops is a viable strategy for restoring soil health in semi-arid regions.

Moisture availability is the primary driver of sequestration magnitude. Irrigated scenarios outperform their rainfed counterparts across both crops, all land covers, and all time horizons in both SOC accumulation and biomass productivity. Where irrigation is feasible, it is the single most impactful management decision for maximizing soil-health outcomes. Residue retention (no-harvest management) provides complementary benefits by returning organic matter to the soil, further amplifying SOC gains, though at the cost of moderately higher soil nitrogen turnover.

Miscanthus consistently outperforms switchgrass in SOC accumulation rate, biomass yield, and soil nitrogen cycling efficiency, primarily because of its superior water-use efficiency, greater below-ground biomass production, and more recalcitrant residue chemistry. On the most productive combination—irrigated miscanthus on shrubland with residue retention—mean $\Delta$SOC at 2100 reaches 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$, a level that represents a substantial improvement in soil fertility and physical structure.

The rate of SOC accumulation declines over time as the soil carbon pool approaches a new equilibrium (Fig. 5), with the most rapid gains occurring in the first two to three decades. Management strategies that sustain continuous high biomass input—particularly irrigation and residue retention—delay this plateau and maintain active soil-health improvement through the end of the simulation period.

A pronounced spatial gradient in SOC response follows the regional rainfall pattern across the basin. The wetter southern portions offer greater soil-health benefits than the drier northern and northeastern areas, highlighting the importance of site-specific water availability when identifying priority parcels for perennial biofuel deployment and soil-health restoration programs.

Future work should quantify the sensitivity of these SOC projections to uncertainty in initial soil conditions and inter-annual climate variability, and evaluate the economic feasibility of perennial biofuel deployment across the site-specific moisture gradient identified here. Parcel-level analysis incorporating local soil surveys may provide additional precision to support land-management decisions.

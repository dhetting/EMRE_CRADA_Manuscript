\date{}

\begin{abstract}
This study characterizes modeled soil organic carbon (SOC), biomass yield, cation exchange capacity (CEC), and N$_2$O responses to land-use change in the Permian Basin of western Texas and southeastern New Mexico. Converting barren land, native shrubland, and rainfed cropland to miscanthus (\textit{Miscanthus} $\times$ \textit{giganteus}) or switchgrass (\textit{Panicum virgatum}) is evaluated under 16 management strategies using DAYCENT, a process-based biogeochemical model applied at 1\,km resolution from 2021 through 2100 with periodically repeated 1980--2020 meteorological forcing. Across all 48 scenario--land-cover combinations, mean modeled $\Delta$SOC (integrated to 105\,cm) ranges from 14 to 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ by year 2100, with a land-use-change increment (SOC$_\text{perennial}$ $-$ SOC$_\text{BAU}$) of 15--84\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$. Among the four management factors in this factorial design, irrigation produces the largest modeled SOC contrast (+26\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ mean main effect). Modeled harvested biomass yield ranges from 1.5 to 6.7\,Mg\,d.m.\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ across harvested scenarios, with irrigated cases 2--3$\times$ higher than rainfed. SOC gains correspond to estimated changes in cation exchange capacity of 0.9 to 6.6\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$ in the top 30\,cm (harvest scenarios: 0.9--3.7; no-harvest scenarios: 1.9--6.6), conditional on a pH-dependent pedotransfer function and validated scenario-specific depth fractions. Under the selected parameterizations, modeled miscanthus scenarios produce higher SOC and yield than switchgrass, reflecting a single productivity-parameter difference. These findings characterize modeled scenario responses under specified assumptions; they do not validate deployment feasibility or represent field-calibrated projections.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} land use change, soil organic carbon, cation exchange capacity, soil health, DAYCENT, Permian Basin

Anthropogenic land use has significantly depleted the global soil organic carbon (SOC) pool, and restoring it is a central strategy for improving soil productivity, biological activity, and long-term land health \citep{Lal2001SoilSequestration,Lal2004bClimateChange,Sanderman2017SoilCarbonDebt,Scharlemann2014GlobalSoilCarbon}. Drylands (arid and semi-arid ecosystems) cover approximately 45\% of Earth's land surface and store roughly 30--40\% of global terrestrial organic carbon \citep{Lal2004aDrylandEcosystems,Schimel2010Drylands}, making them both an opportunity and a challenge for SOC restoration. Field studies show that transitioning barren land and shrubland to higher-biomass vegetation (such as grasslands or perennial crops) increases SOC and improves soil fertility \citep{Arije2024SOCRecovery}. Adopting sustainable land-management practices in cultivated areas, such as conservation agriculture or cover cropping, further raises SOC by promoting higher biomass production \citep{Farage2007Placeholder,He2025Placeholder,Joshi2023Placeholder,Moukanni2022Placeholder}. Limited moisture, alkaline soils that constrain plant growth, and high winds that erode soil and damage herbaceous vegetation all hamper revegetation or agricultural operations in arid lands \citep{DOdorico2012Placeholder,Naorem2023Placeholder}. Perennial biofuel crops provide a mechanism to establish high-biomass vegetation on marginal land and rebuild depleted SOC stocks.


Miscanthus and switchgrass can thrive on marginal lands across a range of moisture conditions, including the semi-arid settings of the Permian Basin, though establishment success and productivity are strongly water-dependent \citep{VanDerWeijde2017Drought,Zeri2013WUE}. Their deep, high-turnover root systems transfer organic carbon to mineral soil horizons, building SOC while above-ground biomass supports bioenergy production \citep{Gelfand2013MarginalLands,AndersonTeixeira2009SOC}.

Miscanthus (\textit{Miscanthus} $\times$ \textit{giganteus}) and switchgrass are well suited for cultivation on marginal lands and compare favorably with conventional biofuel crops such as corn on several measures of soil health and agricultural sustainability. First, their lower fertilizer and water requirements reduce input intensity on already nutrient-limited soils \citep{Davis2009Placeholder,Zeri2013WUE}. Second, both crops slow soil carbon mineralization rates and build SOC, whereas corn cultivation depletes SOC \citep{AndersonTeixeira2009SOC,Ferrarini2022Placeholder}. Third, their year-round ground cover reduces soil erosion and preserves the organic matter that supports soil fertility \citep{Ferrarini2022Placeholder,Smeets2009Performance}. In drylands, these traits make miscanthus and switchgrass candidates for altering modeled soil-C and N$_2$O outcomes under specified scenarios where water availability permits \citep{Zeri2013WUE,VanLoocke2012WUE}.

Perennial biofuel crops can build SOC stocks on marginal land through deep root systems and extensive below-ground biomass \citep{Johnson2007BiomassCropping,Davis2012Agave}. Where harvested, the above-ground biomass supports bioenergy production; this study reports modeled biomass yield for harvested scenarios from DAYCENT \texttt{agcacc} output (see Results). Successful establishment depends on land suitability and water availability \citep{Field2017Placeholder,Kaye2018SoilCarbon}.

As a case in point, the Permian Basin encompasses barren land, native shrubland, and rainfed cropland: three starting land covers that span a wide range of initial SOC states and present different magnitudes of potential SOC gain under conversion to perennial biofuel crops. The basin, located in western Texas and southeastern New Mexico, has a semi-arid climate with hot summers that frequently exceed 32\,°C and cool winters that average 10--15\,°C \citep{PermianBasinClimatePlaceholder}. Annual rainfall remains low, around 300--355\,mm (12--14 inches), and falls sporadically, mainly in late spring and early summer, contributing to frequent drought conditions. Persistent winds drive soil erosion \citep{Naorem2023Placeholder}, and soils vary across the basin but commonly include caliche, sandy soils, clay-rich soils, and saline soils, each of which poses distinct challenges for agriculture and land use \citep{EatonSalinityPlaceholder}.

In this study, DAYCENT biogeochemical modeling evaluates SOC accumulation, cation exchange capacity, and soil-nitrogen dynamics across 16 management scenarios and three starting land covers in the Permian Basin. No prior published study has applied DAYCENT at 1\,km resolution to simulate soil carbon outcomes under land-use change to perennial biofuel crops across the Permian Basin. Results do not extend to other soil health indicators such as aggregate stability, microbial biomass, or water-holding capacity, which require separate measurement.

\subsection*{Study area}

The study area covers 182,575\,km$^2$ of the Permian Basin delineated at 1\,km resolution: 158,852\,km$^2$ of shrubland, 23,609\,km$^2$ of rainfed cropland, and 114\,km$^2$ of barren land \citep{PermianBasinBoundaryUSGS}.

Shrubland occupies 87\,\% of the study area by pixel count, so area-weighted summary statistics are dominated by shrubland outcomes.

\subsection*{Management scenarios}

Each of the 16 scenarios combines two levels of nitrogen fertilization (fertilized: 12\,g\,N\,m$^{-2}$\,yr$^{-1}$ = 120\,kg\,N\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ applied once annually as mineral nitrogen; unfertilized: no mineral nitrogen fertilizer inputs), two irrigation regimes (irrigated: automatic application triggered when soil water content drops to 50\,\% of plant-available water-holding capacity, with repeat events throughout the growing season; rainfed: no supplemental water), and two harvest regimes (harvested: above-ground biomass removed at the end of the growing season; no-harvest: residues retained on the soil surface). Fertilizer rates and irrigation triggers were established by Wang et al. \citep{WangDou2017DAYCENT,WangDou2020AgronomyJ} for bioenergy crop simulations in the Southern Great Plains and are reported directly in the schedule files archived with this study.

The soil profile is parameterized to 210\,cm depth across 14 layers (0--2, 2--5, 5--10, 10--20, 20--30, 30--45, 45--60, 60--75, 75--90, 90--105, 105--120, 120--150, 150--180, 180--210\,cm), as specified in the \texttt{soils.in} parameter file. Root density is distributed down to approximately 105\,cm. DAYCENT tracks soil organic matter pools (active, slow, and passive fractions) across all layers where root density is non-zero; the reported $\Delta$SOC therefore integrates the full root zone to 105\,cm depth.

\textbf{CEC calculation.}
Change in cation exchange capacity is estimated from modeled $\Delta$SOC using a pH-dependent pedotransfer function applied to the top 30\,cm (the agronomically active zone where most root-nutrient exchange occurs). The mass-weighted change in CEC for the 0--30\,cm layer is:

\[
\Delta \mathrm{CEC}_{0-30}
= \frac{b(\mathrm{pH}) \cdot \Delta \mathrm{OM}_{0-30}}{M_{\mathrm{soil},0-30}},
\]

\noindent where $b(\mathrm{pH}) = 30 \times \mathrm{pH}$ (cmol$_\text{c}$ kg$^{-1}$ OM) is the pH-dependent organic-matter CEC coefficient (a linear approximation valid over pH 5.5--8.5 for temperate mineral soils \citep{BradyWeilSoilsPlaceholder}); $\Delta \mathrm{OM}_{0-30} = \Delta\mathrm{SOC}_{0-30} \times 1.724$ (g OM ha$^{-1}$) converts carbon to organic matter using the Van Bemmelen factor; and $M_{\mathrm{soil},0-30} = \rho_b \times 30 \times 10^{8}$ (g ha$^{-1}$) is the dry soil mass in the layer, with bulk density $\rho_b$ from gSSURGO (\texttt{dbthirdbar}) per pixel. $\Delta\mathrm{SOC}_{0-30}$ is estimated from modeled $\Delta\mathrm{SOC}_{0-105}$ using scenario-specific depth fractions validated by full-basin AutoDAYCENT re-runs (182,575 pixels across all three starting land covers) with per-layer SOM output ($\texttt{som1c}$, $\texttt{som2c}$, $\texttt{metabc}$, $\texttt{strmac}$ for layers 0--10 and 10--30\,cm). The validated depth fraction differs systematically by harvest management: harvest scenarios have $\Delta\mathrm{SOC}_{0-30} / \Delta\mathrm{SOC}_{0-105}$ of 0.60--0.68 (LC-averaged mean 0.648), consistent with root-biomass-driven accumulation distributed across the full profile; no-harvest scenarios have fractions of 0.86--1.09 (LC-averaged mean 0.952), reflecting surface residue decomposition that preferentially builds topsoil carbon. Shrubland no-harvest fractions ($>$\,1.0) indicate net loss in deeper layers while topsoil gains; cultivated no-harvest fractions (0.86--0.89) remain below unity due to the different soil history. The passive SOM pool (\texttt{som3c}) was not available as a layer-specific output and was omitted; its long turnover time ($>$\,1000\,yr) implies negligible contribution to inter-decadal $\Delta\mathrm{SOC}$. Per-pixel pH is from gSSURGO (\texttt{ph1to1h2o}) and is treated as static. The clay contribution to CEC (CEC$_\text{clay}$ = 25\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$) does not change with land use and is not included in $\Delta$CEC.

\textbf{Crop parameterization.}
DAYCENT crop parameters for miscanthus (\texttt{MISC}) and switchgrass (\texttt{SG3}) are specified in \texttt{crop.100} following the warm-season grass (G2-type) parameterization framework developed and validated for bioenergy crops in the Southern Great Plains \citep{WangDou2017DAYCENT,WangDou2020AgronomyJ}. The corresponding DAYCENT schedule scenarios are labeled \texttt{misc\_*} and \texttt{swi\_*} for brevity. Wang led the calibration of this parameter set for bioenergy crops in Texas in collaboration with DAYCENT developers Paustian and Del Grosso. The miscanthus crop type draws additionally on DAYCENT parameter sets validated against measured SOC and biomass yield at the Energy Farm in Illinois \citep{Davis2010Comparative}.

\textbf{Limitation: parameter transfer and species specificity.}
No site-specific calibration against Permian Basin field measurements of SOC or biomass yield was conducted. The \texttt{MISC} crop type is derived from a switchgrass/warm-season grass (G2) base parameterization rather than a dedicated miscanthus field calibration. This means that mechanistic explanations for the modeled miscanthus--switchgrass SOC gap (e.g., lignin content, root turnover, residue chemistry) are supported by published biological literature but are not directly captured in the model parameterization difference. The modeled SOC gap between the two crop parameterizations should be interpreted as a parameterization-specific scenario outcome, not a validated biological conclusion. No independent SOC validation against Permian Basin field measurements was conducted.

\subsection*{Analytical workflow}

This study assembles the inputs that DAYCENT requires for regional simulations at 1\,km resolution, assuming land-use change begins in 2021 and continues through 2100, and applies a single factorial study design (2 crops $\times$ 2 fertilization levels $\times$ 2 irrigation regimes $\times$ 2 harvest regimes = 16 management scenarios) to each of three starting land covers: barren, shrubland, and rainfed cropland (\texttt{cultivated\_rfd}). Figure 2 summarizes the spatial distribution of the starting land covers and the factorial structure of the 16 scenarios.

DAYCENT (Daily Century, DDcentEVI revision 279), developed by \citet{Parton1998DAYCENT}, is a widely used process-based biogeochemical model that simulates the interactions among climate, soil, and vegetation in terrestrial ecosystems. DAYCENT takes climate data, soil information, vegetation parameters, and land-management practices as inputs and produces net primary production, soil organic carbon dynamics, nitrogen mineralization, leaching, and trace-gas fluxes as outputs \citep{Parton1998DAYCENT,DelGrosso2005Placeholder,DelGrosso2006NationalScale}. The model has been validated against measured N$_2$O flux data at national scale, with model-to-measurement agreement of $r^2 = 0.74$ \citep{DelGrosso2005Placeholder}, supporting its use for nitrogen cycling projections in this analysis. Numerous studies have validated and applied the model across agroecosystem contexts \citep{Davis2009Placeholder,Jarecki2020Placeholder,Laub2024Placeholder}. DAYMET daily gridded meteorological data (1980--2020) from DOE ORNL supplies weather inputs \citep{Thornton2022Daymet}. Soil-property inputs are extracted from the gridded Soil Survey Geographic Database (gSSURGO) produced by USDA NRCS \citep{NRCSgSSURGOPlaceholder}. The starting land-cover classification is derived from the USDA NASS Cropland Data Layer (CDL) \citep{NASCDLPlaceholder}.

Each land cover goes through a multi-century equilibrium spin-up before the land-use-change simulation begins: barren and shrubland sites spin up from 3150\,BCE to 2020\,CE, and cropland sites spin up from 3150\,BCE through 1850 then follow a historical cropping baseline from 1851 to 2020. The cropland baseline represents dryland cotton (\textit{Gossypium hirsutum}) production with progressive intensification from 1851 to 2020: tillage transitions from minimal disturbance to conventional tillage, nitrogen fertilization increases in five stages from zero to 120\,kg\,N\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ (matching modern rates by 2001), and residue return gradually increases from 10\% to 50\% of above-ground biomass retained \citep{WangDAYCENTArchivePlaceholder}. This historical baseline reflects the cotton agriculture that has dominated rainfed cropland in the Permian Basin since the late 19th century. The spin-up and baseline phases stabilize initial SOC pools before land-use change begins in 2021.

\textbf{Management schedule design.} Each of the 16 land-use-change scenarios shares a common annual management structure: crop establishment on day 131 (May 11); nitrogen fertilizer application at 120\,kg\,N\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ on day 161 (June 10) for fertilized scenarios (0\,kg\,N\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ for unfertilized scenarios); automatic irrigation triggered at 50\,\% available water-holding capacity for irrigated scenarios (no irrigation for rainfed scenarios); and end-of-season senescence on day 300 (October 27). Harvest scenarios remove all standing biomass on day 300 (\texttt{HARV ALL}); no-harvest scenarios allow senesced material to fall as surface residue (\texttt{SENM}), followed by a stand-maintenance cultivation event on day 330. BAU continuation scenarios maintain each starting land cover (shrubland, cotton cropland, barren) without crop conversion through 2100 under the same meteorological sequence.

\textbf{Limitation: climate forcing design.} Daily 1980--2020 DAYMET meteorological data are periodically repeated through 2100 as the climate forcing sequence. This design preserves historical seasonal and interannual variability within the repeated period but excludes secular climate trends and future changes in variability. The simulations therefore characterize potential SOC and N outcomes under periodically repeated historical meteorology; they do not project outcomes under projected future climate conditions.

\textbf{CO$_2$ forcing.} Atmospheric CO$_2$ fertilization effects were disabled in all 16 scenarios (CO$_2$ Systems = $-1$ in every schedule file). Plant physiology parameters reflect ambient-CO$_2$ conditions; the model does not apply CO$_2$-driven stimulation of productivity or transpiration efficiency through 2100. This is a conservative assumption that may underestimate SOC accumulation under rising atmospheric CO$_2$.

\section*{Results and discussion}

Under the selected crop parameterizations, modeled miscanthus scenarios produce higher SOC accumulation and greater CEC gains than modeled switchgrass scenarios across all scenario comparisons. The two crop types differ in a single DAYCENT \texttt{crop.100} parameter: the potential production slope PRDX(1) is 3.5 for MISC and 2.75 for SG3 (switchgrass), both derived from the G2 warm-season grass framework; all other parameters are identical. Among the four management factors, irrigation produces the largest modeled SOC contrast (+26.1\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ mean main effect at 2100), followed by residue retention (+12.4\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$), crop type (+9.0\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$), and fertilization (+3.5\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$). Irrigated scenarios throughout this paper are water-unconstrained model cases; they do not establish the feasibility of irrigation given water availability, infrastructure, or economic constraints in the Permian Basin. No-harvest scenarios retain all above-ground biomass as surface residue; they are included to isolate the effect of residue management on SOC dynamics and do not represent an operational bioenergy production strategy.

SOC rises in all scenarios (Figs. 3 and 4), consistent with prior reports \citep{Dohleman2009Miscanthus,Iqbal2015Placeholder,Jarecki2020Placeholder} that perennial biofuel crops sequester SOC relative to degraded or cultivated baselines. Across all 48 scenario--land-cover combinations, mean $\Delta$SOC (integrated to 105\,cm depth, relative to the 2021 initial state) at year 2100 ranges from 14 to 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$, with the largest modeled gains under miscanthus cultivation on shrubland with irrigation and residue retention. BAU continuation runs (each starting land cover maintained without crop conversion through 2100) show that BAU SOC stocks remain nearly stable over the simulation period (shrubland: +0.95\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$; cropland: $-1.25$\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$; barren: +0.64\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ at 2100). The barren land cover contributes only 114 pixels (less than 0.1\,\% of the study area); its scenario-mean range (16--70\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$) falls within the range of the two dominant land covers. Expressed as endpoint-based mean net SOC change per simulation year (2021--2100 divided by 79 years), the scenario-mean rates correspond to 0.18 to 1.08\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$. These rates average the non-linear trajectory; scenario-specific annual rates are captured in Fig. 5. Published DAYCENT simulations and field studies of miscanthus and switchgrass on marginal lands in the US Midwest report annual SOC accumulation rates of approximately 0.2--1.0\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ under more humid conditions. \citet{Gelfand2013MarginalLands} report 0.59\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ for miscanthus and 0.23\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ for switchgrass on marginal lands; DAYCENT simulations for Illinois conditions report miscanthus rates near 0.9--1.0\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ \citep{Davis2010Comparative,Jarecki2020Placeholder}. Our Permian Basin rainfed scenarios fall below these humid-region benchmarks, and our irrigated scenarios overlap the lower end of the published range, consistent with semi-arid water limitation. Field measurements from perennial bioenergy crops in temperate settings show cumulative SOC gains of approximately 5--30\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ over 5--20 years \citep{AndersonTeixeira2009SOC,Gelfand2013MarginalLands}, placing our 79-year model projections in a plausible range.

Across scenarios, miscanthus accumulates more SOC than switchgrass. In the model, this difference is driven entirely by the productivity parameter PRDX(1) (3.5 for MISC vs.\ 2.75 for SG3); literature attributes such differences to miscanthus producing more below-ground biomass, its lignocellulosic residues decomposing more slowly, and its root system delivering organic carbon to deeper soil layers \citep{Gelfand2013MarginalLands,Davis2010Comparative,Fu2022MiscanthusSOC}. The mechanistic literature provides context for the observed pattern but does not validate it, because the model captures differences through productivity alone, not through species-specific residue chemistry or root architecture.

% NOTE [parameterization caveat moved to body text above and Results opener]

Switchgrass produces more fine-root biomass than miscanthus, yet its total below-ground biomass is about half that of miscanthus \citep{Chimento2015Placeholder,Martani2021Placeholder}. Water stress reduces switchgrass below-ground biomass more than miscanthus because of switchgrass's lower water-use efficiency \citep{VanLoocke2012WUE,Zeri2013WUE}. Below-ground biomass also decomposes more slowly in miscanthus than in switchgrass, providing a longer-lasting SOC input \citep{Fu2022MiscanthusSOC,Xu2024Placeholder}.

Greater below-ground carbon inputs and slower residue decomposition in miscanthus than in switchgrass result in the larger modeled SOC accumulation observed across miscanthus scenarios.

When aboveground biomass is not harvested (crop-residue retention), SOC increases through several mechanisms. Unharvested residues are a continuous source of organic matter that gradually decomposes and enriches SOC \citep{Mann2012Placeholder}. Residues on the soil surface also reduce wind and water erosion, limiting loss of existing SOC and preserving topsoil structure \citep{BlancoCanqui2016Placeholder}. In addition, residues provide substrate for soil microorganisms, which accelerates incorporation of organic carbon into stable soil aggregates \citep{Lal2005ForestSoils,SixPlaceholder2002}. Crop-residue retention thus promotes improvements in soil fertility, water-holding capacity, and aggregate stability that have been documented in field studies \citep{BlancoCanaquisPlaceholder2011}.

Higher inputs of irrigation and nitrogen fertilization also raise SOC through stimulation of plant growth. Irrigation enhances productivity and increases above- and below-ground biomass inputs to the soil \citep{Wang2018Placeholder}. The additional organic matter (root biomass and crop residues) provides substrate for microbial activity and accelerates incorporation of carbon into stable soil fractions \citep{Basso2013Placeholder}. Nitrogen fertilization stimulates plant growth similarly, transferring more organic carbon to the soil through root exudates and residues \citep{Liu2016Placeholder}. These inputs augment SOC.

\subsection*{SOC accumulation over time}

\manufig{figures/figure5.png}{Annual trajectory of modeled $\Delta$SOC (Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$, integrated to 105\,cm depth) by scenario and starting land cover, 2021--2100. Lines show the pixel-mean; ribbons span the 25th--75th percentile.}

The rate of SOC accumulation is highest early in the simulation and declines over time as the soil carbon pool approaches a new equilibrium (Fig. 5). Early accumulation is driven by the transition from low-productivity baseline land covers to high-biomass perennial systems, which rapidly increases organic-matter inputs. As the pool deepens, labile carbon fractions (those that turn over on timescales of months to a few years) cycle more rapidly and contribute less to net SOC gain, while recalcitrant fractions accumulate more slowly but persist longer \citep{Parton1987Placeholder,VonLutzow2007Placeholder}. This shift in pool composition is consistent with the flattening of the SOC trajectory across all scenarios by mid-century.

Management strategies that maintain high and continuous biomass input (irrigation and residue retention in particular) delay this plateau and sustain elevated SOC accumulation rates through 2100 (Fig. 5).

Harvested biomass yield ranges from 1.5 to 6.7\,Mg\,d.m.\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ across the eight harvest scenarios and two primary land covers at year 2100 (Fig. 6A). Irrigated scenarios average 5.2\,Mg\,d.m.\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$ and rainfed scenarios 1.7\,Mg\,d.m.\,ha$^{-1}$\,yr$^{-1}$, consistent with water being the primary yield-limiting factor in the semi-arid Permian Basin. Rainfed yield is comparable to reported yields of switchgrass on marginal lands under rainfed conditions in drier US regions \citep{VanDerWeijde2017Drought}. Miscanthus scenarios produce approximately 25\,\% higher yield than switchgrass under equivalent management, attributable to the higher PRDX(1) productivity parameter. \subsection*{Modeled irrigation demand}

% NOTE: yield data confirmed available from agcacc in harvest_*.h5 files.

Irrigation is the dominant modeled control on SOC accumulation, with a main effect of +26.1\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ at 2100 (mean across all scenario--land-cover combinations), operating through soil water availability. The residue-retention effect (+12.4\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$) and crop-type effect (+9.0\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$, MISC vs.\ SWI) are smaller but agronomically relevant. Fertilization contributes the smallest main effect (+3.5\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$). Irrigation demand in the model averages 3.4\,cm\,yr$^{-1}$ (interquartile range 2.8--4.0\,cm\,yr$^{-1}$) for irrigated scenarios at year 2100, based on the DAYCENT \texttt{irrtot} annual totals. These are water-unconstrained scenarios driven by a soil-water trigger at 50\,\% available water-holding capacity; they do not account for water availability, aquifer depletion, pumping energy, or infrastructure in the Permian Basin. Irrigated cases should be interpreted as conditional modeled upper bounds, not as operational recommendations.

\subsection*{Soil nitrogen dynamics}

Nitrogen fertilization raises soil N$_2$O flux more than irrigation does, acting through distinct mechanisms. N$_2$O flux reflects gaseous nitrogen loss through nitrification and denitrification. Nitrogen fertilizers increase soil nitrogen availability, boosting microbial nitrification and denitrification activity that converts mineral nitrogen to N$_2$O as a byproduct \citep{Bouwman1996Placeholder,Bouwman2002Placeholder,ButterbachBahl2013Placeholder,LiuGreaver2009Placeholder}. Irrigation, by contrast, may moderate N$_2$O flux under some conditions by maintaining more uniform soil moisture; however, this directional effect is conditional on soil properties and saturation regime and is not universal \citep{Groffman2009Placeholder,Zhu2013Placeholder}. Across the 16 scenarios and two primary land covers at year 2100, mean N$_2$O flux ranges from 0.011\,g\,N$_2$O-N\,m$^{-2}$\,yr$^{-1}$ in unfertilized rainfed harvested switchgrass on shrubland to 0.068\,g\,N$_2$O-N\,m$^{-2}$\,yr$^{-1}$ in fertilized irrigated no-harvest miscanthus on rainfed cropland; fertilized scenarios average approximately 2-fold higher flux than unfertilized scenarios. Annual N$_2$O trajectories are shown in Fig. 6B; cumulative N$_2$O-N flux over 2021--2100 ranges from 10 to 59\,kg\,N$_2$O-N\,ha$^{-1}$ across scenarios, with fertilization the dominant driver (fertilized mean 42\,kg\,N$_2$O-N\,ha$^{-1}$ vs. unfertilized 19\,kg\,N$_2$O-N\,ha$^{-1}$) and irrigation secondary (irrigated mean 37 vs. rainfed 25\,kg\,N$_2$O-N\,ha$^{-1}$). These cumulative totals represent the modeled 79-year N loss from the system; they do not constitute a full nitrogen balance without corresponding inputs and other loss pathways.

No-harvest management retains crop residues on the soil surface, which increases soil organic matter and creates conditions that elevate nitrogen turnover. The organic matter and residual nitrogen in unharvested residues provide substrate for denitrifying bacteria in anaerobic microsites, increasing N$_2$O flux relative to harvested scenarios \citep{Smith2008GHGMitigation,VerhoefPlaceholder2006}. Harvesting removes a significant portion of the organic residues, limiting the denitrification substrate and modeled N$_2$O production. The trade-off is that harvesting also removes organic matter that would otherwise contribute to longer-term SOC accumulation, so the optimal harvest decision depends on whether management priorities emphasize soil nitrogen retention or SOC buildup \citep{BlancoCanqui2016Placeholder}.

The spatial pattern of N$_2$O flux broadly mirrors that of SOC accumulation: wetter southern portions of the basin show higher N$_2$O flux under fertilized scenarios because greater overall microbial activity and soil nitrogen cycling occur in more productive environments.

Soil organic carbon accumulation raises cation exchange capacity (CEC), improving the soil's ability to retain and supply plant nutrients. CEC calculations here target the top 30\,cm (the agronomically relevant zone where most root activity and nutrient exchange occur) rather than the 105\,cm root zone used for total $\Delta$SOC. Baseline CEC in the Permian Basin study area ranges from 9.6 to 22.5\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$ (25th--75th percentile) across map units, with a median of 15.0\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$ \citep{NRCSgSSURGOPlaceholder}, typical for the semi-arid mixed-clay soils of the region. Land-use conversion to perennial biofuel crops increases CEC through the growth of the organic-matter pool in the top 30\,cm \citep{BradyWeilSoilsPlaceholder}.

At year 2100, mean $\Delta$CEC across the 48 scenario--land-cover combinations ranges from 0.9 to 6.6\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$ (Figs. 7 and 8). The largest gain (6.6\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$, irrigated no-harvest miscanthus on shrubland) and smallest (0.9\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$, rainfed harvested switchgrass on cropland) span the range of soil health improvement the model projects. These changes represent 6--44\,\% of the baseline CEC median, a range that is agronomically significant: higher CEC improves buffering against pH swings, increases retention of calcium, magnesium, and potassium, and reduces leaching losses of applied fertilizers \citep{BradyWeilSoilsPlaceholder}.

The $\Delta$CEC values are estimated by a pH-dependent pedotransfer function and carry uncertainty from (1) the assumed $\textit{CEC}_\text{OM}$ coefficient, which is calibrated for temperate soils and may differ for alkaline semi-arid soils; (2) depth fractions were validated across all 182,575 pixels and all three land covers, but the passive SOM pool (\texttt{som3c}) was excluded from the per-layer sum; (3) the gSSURGO CEC field reflects a laboratory method that may not be directly comparable to PTF-derived values; and (4) approximately 40\,\% of pixels have stored gSSURGO \texttt{ph1to1h2o} values below 5.5 in the H5 resource file---these pixels are clipped to pH\,=\,5.5 in the PTF, making the CEC estimates for those pixels conservative (lower-bound) relative to their probable pH of 6--8.

\section*{Conclusions}

The DAYCENT simulations presented here consistently show that, under periodically repeated historical meteorology and specified crop parameterizations, land-use conversion to perennial grass scenarios produces substantial modeled SOC accumulation across all 48 scenario--land-cover combinations. The scenario-mean $\Delta$SOC at 2100 ranges from 14 to 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ (integrated to 105\,cm depth, relative to the 2021 initial state). BAU continuation runs confirm that BAU SOC stocks change by less than 1.3\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ by 2100 for all three starting land covers; the true land-use-change increment (SOC$_\text{perennial}$ $-$ SOC$_\text{BAU}$) therefore ranges from 15 to 84\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$, essentially identical to the from-initial-state values. The gSSURGO 0--30\,cm baseline of 20.7\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ cannot be directly compared to this 0--105\,cm $\Delta$SOC because the depth intervals differ; a fully matched comparison would require gSSURGO SOC stocks integrated to 0--105\,cm (the Valu1 table provides only 0--30 and 0--100\,cm options). Area-weighted over the study region (87\,\% shrubland), the scenario range narrows to 20--81\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$. The corresponding estimated CEC increases (0.9--6.6\,cmol$_\text{c}$\,kg$^{-1}$; harvest 0.9--3.7, no-harvest 1.9--6.6, conditional on validated depth fractions and pH-dependent PTF) are agronomically significant relative to the baseline median, with harvest scenarios well-constrained and no-harvest estimates reflecting validated top-heavy SOC accumulation.

Among the four management factors, irrigation is the dominant modeled driver of SOC accumulation (main effect +26.1\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ at 2100), followed by residue retention (+12.4\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$), crop type (+9.0\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$), and fertilization (+3.5\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$). Irrigated scenarios produce greater SOC accumulation than rainfed counterparts in all land covers and across both crop parameterizations. Irrigated scenarios are water-unconstrained model cases; they do not establish the feasibility of irrigation in the Permian Basin. Residue retention (no-harvest management) amplifies SOC gains at the cost of moderately higher N$_2$O flux.

Under the selected parameterizations, modeled miscanthus scenarios produce higher SOC accumulation than modeled switchgrass scenarios. The two crop types differ in a single \texttt{crop.100} parameter: the potential production slope PRDX(1) is 3.5 for MISC vs.\ 2.75 for SG3 (switchgrass), representing a 27\,\% higher productivity ceiling; all other parameters are identical between the two crops. On the highest-SOC combination (irrigated no-harvest miscanthus on shrubland), mean $\Delta$SOC reaches 85\,Mg\,C\,ha$^{-1}$ at year 2100. This difference should be interpreted as a model-scenario outcome driven by the productivity parameter, not a validated biological conclusion.

SOC accumulation rates are highest in the first two to three decades and decline as the soil carbon pool approaches a new equilibrium. Management strategies that sustain continuous biomass input (irrigation and residue retention) delay this plateau. Modeled responses follow the basin's rainfall gradient, with the wetter southern portions showing consistently higher SOC gains.

These findings characterize modeled SOC, biomass yield, CEC, and N$_2$O responses under a specific set of DAYCENT scenario assumptions. Several limitations constrain transferability to deployment decisions: (1) no Permian Basin field calibration of SOC or yield was performed; (2) climate forcing omits secular trends and CO$_2$ fertilization effects are disabled; (3) irrigation demand and water-resource feasibility are not evaluated; (4) CEC depth fractions are validated from the full 182,575-pixel basin-wide per-layer re-run; remaining uncertainty comes from the excluded passive SOM pool (\texttt{som3c}) and PTF applicability to semi-arid alkaline soils; (5) DAYCENT does not simulate a crop establishment phase, so early-year yields may be overestimated; (6) the gSSURGO 0--30\,cm baseline cannot be compared to the 0--105\,cm DAYCENT $\Delta$SOC without a matched-depth baseline; the gSSURGO Valu1 table provides SOC stocks for 0--30\,cm and 0--100\,cm intervals but not 0--105\,cm. Future work should address field calibration, climate uncertainty, water-resource constraints, and establishment-phase yield dynamics before drawing management recommendations.
